Water usage

Our current uses and the potential of water.

Hydropower

Hydropower is the most common and well-known use of water for energy. It involves harnessing the energy of flowing or falling water, such as rivers or waterfalls, to generate electricity.

Hydropower plants use turbines to convert the kinetic energy of moving water into mechanical energy, which is then converted into electrical energy.

The infrastructure needed to create hydropower can also impact on ecosystems in adverse ways. For this reason, many consider such small-scale operations a more environmentally-friendly option.

Tidal and wave power

Tidal power harnesses the energy from the rise and fall of tides in coastal areas. It involves constructing barrages or turbines that capture the kinetic energy of tidal currents a nd convert it into electricity. Tidal power has the advantage of being predictable and highly reliable.

Wave power captures the energy of ocean waves and converts it into electricity. Various technologies, such as floating devices or underwater oscillating water columns, are used to harness the wave energy and drive generators. Wave power has the potential to provide a significant source of renewable energy.

water reservoirs

Reservoirs act as a reliable water source, especially in areas that experience seasonal variations in rainfall or face water scarcity issues. They store water during wet periods and provide a steady supply during dry seasons, ensuring a consistent water source for domestic, industrial, agricultural, and municipal needs.

Reservoirs also help mitigate the risk of flooding by capturing excess water during heavy rainfall events. By regulating the release of stored water, reservoirs can control downstream flows, reducing the likelihood of downstream flooding and associated damages to infrastructure and communities.
